My Journey into Tech

Drop me a line

I'm a software developer.

I currently live in Colorado but I'm originally from Oklahoma and I've been a fair few places in between.

My first foray into tech was way back in the MySpace days. I loved tinkering with my profile and adding angry teenager music in the background for all to enjoy once they landed on my page. It was fun, but I never really considered it a career. Fast-forward a good few years. After finishing my bachelors, I relocated to NYC and found myself working as a recruiter for C# and C++ developers. I spent my days making cold calls and trying to sell candidates whose skill sets I didn't fully comprehend.

"But James, we're just a mom and pop shop, what exactly would we need a C# developer for?"

"Well Bob, if you have to ask then you are definitely in need of one."

Again, the tech industry was knocking at my door but I didn't get it. The year was 2008, and I had front row seats as things came tumbling down. I decided to change gears, and got myself certified as a teacher from the University of Cambridge. I was on the next flight to Moscow, Russia where I spent the next two and a half years working as teacher. As my career progressed, I took a position in Dusseldorf, Germany as a linguistic consultant and later, on to Saudi Arabia as an advanced instructor.

For those of you who've never been to Saudi Arabia, their (web) infrastructure is somewhat lacking. One night, I was craving a pizza and for the life of me, could not find a single website or phone number to get one. I began to wonder to myself, really? how hard can it be to make a website? So, I decided to give it a try.

I did a few tutorials and started dabbling. I fell in love with code. It finally dawned on me that this could be real career. So, after looking for the longest, most-intense code school I could find, I got accepted to the Turing School in Denver and packed my bags.

*Pro Tip* Don't tell your significant other you're quitting your job and moving around the world 6 weeks after having a child.

I spent seven long months in a basement, putting in 12-14 hour days to learn the craft. Our time was measured in sprints and 7-11 runs. I had the opportunity to work with some remarkable people who changed the way I look at problems, and make really cool apps in the process. I went from not knowing how to create a new directory in the terminal to building multi-faceted, fully-tested, production-level web apps.

I now have a career that I love and cannot imagine myself ever doing anything else.

Swept Away

Swept Away is an app that was inspired by those diligent civil servants handing out parking tickets. It is a directory for the street sweeping routes and schedules of the city of Boston. Once logging in with their Google account, users can visit the index page which displays every route, its scheduled day and time, and which side of the street will be swept. Alternatively, they can search by street name limiting the results. After clicking on a individual route, users can view a Google map rendering the specifics of that route.

This was a solo project that uses a Ruby 2.4/Rails 5 backend and the Faraday gem to hit the City of Boston's API. It makes use of Google's OAuth service to log in and Google Maps to provide a visual representation of routes. The Kaminari gem was utilized for pagination and Javascript/jQuery was implemented for the filter function. A PostgreSQL database was used to store user data and tokens. It was completed in a four-day sprint in April of 2017. Future iterations will include a mailer to provide users with reminders.

CloneBnB

This app is a clone of the popular accommodation booking site, AirBnB. After registration, users become a 'traveler' and can view/book properties in a number of different cities. Travelers can use the internal messaging service to contact hosts with questions before booking a property. After their stay has been confirmed and completed, users can leave reviews about places that they have stayed. They also have the opportunity to register as a 'host' and list their their own property for booking.


This was a four-person team project that uses a Ruby 2.4/Rails 5 backend. We utilized the Active Model Serializer gem to serialize an internal API that provides administrators with business intelligence data via ActiveRecord and SQL calls. The Faker gem was used to generate ~5000 records in the PostgreSQL database. It makes use of the BCrypt gem for authentication and authorization as well as Twilio to receive an SMS in the case of forgotten passwords. This is a multi-tenancy app the gives the user priveleges based on 4 different roles (unautheticated, traveler, host, and administrator) and permissions were 'handrolled.' It was completed in a two-week sprint in April of 2017.

PogHogs

The PogHogs app was built as an online marketplace for the 90's pog craze. Users have the ability to add items to cart and purchase as well as leave reviews about products they've bought while admins can manage stock levels, order/status and user priveleges .

This was a three-person team project that uses a Ruby 2.4/Rails 5 backend. Products are handled in a PostgreSQL database with images on paperclip. The app utilizes the Bootstrap library for styling. It was completed in a two-week sprint in February of 2017.

    • Howler

    The Howler app was designed to allow users to analyze the sentiment of any text using scores of 13 different tones. If users sign in with their Twitter account, they can save their analysis and compare multiple texts.

    This was a five-person team, brownfield project that uses a Ruby 2.4/Rails 5 backend combined with JavaScript, jQuery, and AJAX calls to render charts, dynamically edit, and compare data. It makes use of Twitter's OAuth service to log in and a PostgreSQL database to store user data. The Watson API is implemented to provide tone analysis as well as Highcharts to render data in a user-friendly manner. It was completed in a two four-day sprints in May of 2017.

    APICurious

    APICurious is a personalized GitHub interface that provides users' with highlights reel of their GitHub activity without any of that pesky code. User's can access a list of their lastest commits, repositories, organizations, followers, people following them, as well as all GitHub activity for them. There's also a link to the real GitHub in case you feel like doing some actual work.


    This was a solo project that uses a Ruby 2.4/Rails 5 backend and the Faraday gem to hit GitHub's API. It makes use of GitHub's OAuth service to log in and provide a user token that's attached to query parameters. A PostgreSQL database was used to store user data. It was completed in a four-day sprint in March of 2017.

    URLockbox

    The URLockbox is an app for tracking your favorite links. It utilizes BCrypt which requires users to create an account with their email address and a password in order to keep track of their links. Once signed in, users must enter a valid URL (https://www.google.com rather than google.com) and a user-chosen title (cannot be blank) to save. Links have an 'unread' status by default but can be changed using the 'Mark as Read/Mark as Unread' buttons. Users can filter their links in one of two ways. The first is to enter text into the filter box, as a user types, their results list will shorten with each key typed. The second is to click on the "Only Read Links" or "Only Unread Links" buttons which limit results to the read status of each link. This app also works in conjunction with HotReads, which tracks the most popular links across all users in the database.


    This was a solo project that uses a Ruby 2.4/Rails 5 backend and ansynchronous JavaScript to provide a seemless user experience without needing to reload. It makes use of the BCrypt gem for authentication and authorization as well as the validate_url gem to ensure the validity of web addresses. The sister app (HotReads) also employs the rack_cors gem in order to overcome the same origin policy during AJAX calls. It was completed in a two-day sprint in June of 2017.
